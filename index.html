The Dynamics of Local Business Survival and Social Impact in San Diego

Understanding business survival patterns through data-driven analysis (1990-2024)

Explore the Project

About the Project

üìä

Mission

To understand business survival in San Diego and examine how often local businesses fail over time, identifying communities at risk and factors contributing to business instability.

üéØ

Objective

Analyze trends in business openings, closures, and neighborhood-level economic conditions to provide actionable insights for policymakers and economic development organizations.

üí°

Impact

Help communities understand where business failure is most common and how targeted interventions can support long-term economic resilience in San Diego.

Data Sources

  • City of San Diego Open Data Portal: Business registrations and closures (1990-2024)
  • Federal Open-Data Portals: Unemployment rates and economic indicators
  • Geographic Data: Neighborhood boundaries, ZIP codes, council districts
  • Industry Classification: NAICS codes and sector descriptions

Step-by-Step Analysis

1

Data Collection & Integration

What we did: Gathered and merged five separate datasets covering active businesses and inactive businesses across four time periods (1990-2000, 2000-2010, 2010-2015, 2015-current).

Dataset Overview:

  • Active businesses: Current operating businesses
  • Historical inactive: 4 datasets spanning 35 years
  • Total records: 400,000+ business entries
  • Geographic coverage: All San Diego County ZIP codes
2

Data Cleaning & Standardization

What we did: Cleaned and standardized column names, converted date fields, extracted ZIP codes, and created consistent business status flags.

Key Transformations:

  • Standardized column names to lowercase with underscores
  • Converted 4 date columns to datetime format
  • Extracted clean 5-digit ZIP codes for geographic analysis
  • Created boolean is_active flag for business status
  • Removed duplicates and records with missing critical fields
3

Geographic Enrichment

What we did: Mapped 80+ ZIP codes to specific San Diego neighborhoods and regional clusters for spatial analysis.

North County

Carlsbad, Oceanside, Escondido, San Marcos, Vista, Poway

Central

Downtown, Hillcrest, North Park, Mission Valley, City Heights

East County

El Cajon, Santee, La Mesa, Alpine, Lakeside

South Bay

Chula Vista, National City, Imperial Beach, San Ysidro

West/Coastal

Point Loma, Ocean Beach, Pacific Beach, La Jolla

4

Industry Classification

What we did: Categorized businesses using NAICS codes into industry sectors and created higher-level industry clusters.

5

Survival Metrics Calculation

What we did: Calculated business duration, closure dates, and survival flags for each business.

Key Metrics Developed:

  • Closure Date: Date of business termination (from certificate expiration)
  • Duration Years: Time from business start to closure or reference date (2025-01-01)
  • Survival Flag: Binary indicator (1 = active, 0 = closed)
  • Start Year: Year business began operations
  • Closure Year: Year business closed (if applicable)
6

Unemployment Correlation Analysis

What we did: Integrated San Diego unemployment data and calculated correlations with business closure rates.

Pearson Correlation

r = 0.27

p-value = 0.12

Modest positive linear relationship (not statistically significant at 5% level)

Spearman Correlation

r = 0.34

p-value = 0.044

Significant monotonic relationship - higher unemployment correlates with higher closures

7

Time-Series Analysis

What we did: Calculated yearly closure rates from 1990-2024 by analyzing active business base and annual closures.

Closure Rate Formula:

For each year:

  • Active Base: Businesses started on/before that year and not yet closed
  • Closures: Businesses that closed in that specific year
  • Closure Rate: Closures √∑ Active Base
8

Sector-Level Analysis

What we did: Computed closure rates for each NAICS sector across all years to identify industry-specific patterns.

Analysis Outputs Generated:

  • sector_year_closure_rate.csv - Closure rates by sector and year
  • sector_year_num_closures.csv - Absolute closure counts
  • industry_cluster_year_closure_rate.csv - Higher-level cluster analysis
  • yearly_closure_unemployment.csv - Merged economic indicators
9

Kaplan-Meier Survival Analysis

What we did: Used survival analysis techniques to model business longevity and create survival probability curves.

Survival Analysis Insights:

  • Overall survival curves showing probability of remaining active over time
  • Sector-specific survival curves for top 6 industries
  • Identification of critical "valley of death" period (years 1-5)
  • ~50% of businesses survive past 5 years
10

Cox Proportional Hazards Modeling

What we did: Built multivariate regression model to quantify sector-specific and temporal effects on business closure risk.

Model Specification:

  • Outcome: Time until closure (duration_years) with event indicator
  • Covariates: Start year (continuous) + Sector (categorical)
  • Key Finding: Strong heterogeneity in closure hazard across sectors
  • Temporal Effect: Recent cohorts have slightly lower closure hazard

Key Findings

üìâ

Economic Correlation

Statistically significant correlation (Spearman r = 0.34, p = 0.044) between unemployment rates and business closure rates. Economic downturns increase closure risk, but multiple factors influence business survival.

üìÖ

Temporal Patterns

Most closures occur within first 3-5 years ("valley of death"). Recent business cohorts show improved survival rates. Major closure spikes align with recessions (2001, 2008-2009, COVID-19).

üè¢

Sector Vulnerability

High-risk: Accommodation & Food Services, Retail Trade, Arts & Entertainment
Stable: Professional Services, Healthcare, Finance & Insurance

üó∫Ô∏è

Geographic Disparities

North County shows generally stable business environment. South Bay and parts of East County experience higher closure rates. Central San Diego has high density with mixed survival outcomes.

‚è±Ô∏è

Critical Period

First 5 years are critical for business survival. Approximately 50% of businesses survive past this threshold. Businesses surviving beyond 5 years show significantly improved long-term prospects.

üìä

Statistical Significance

Cox regression model confirms strong sector heterogeneity independent of time effects. Some sectors have 2-3x higher closure hazard than others, controlling for cohort effects.

Recommendations & Impact

For Policymakers

  • Launch "First Five Years" support program targeting critical early-stage period
  • Create sector-specific intervention plans for high-risk industries
  • Develop economic shock response fund for unemployment spike periods
  • Implement geographic equity initiatives in vulnerable neighborhoods
  • Establish quarterly business health monitoring system

For Economic Development Organizations

  • Build sector-specific mentorship programs pairing new with established businesses
  • Deploy targeted resources to South Bay and East County areas
  • Create real-time business health dashboard with predictive analytics
  • Offer subsidized consulting services during startup phase
  • Facilitate industry cluster initiatives for peer support

For Community Organizations

  • Strengthen neighborhood business associations and networks
  • Expand microfinance and community lending programs
  • Organize regular networking and peer learning events
  • Create collective purchasing programs to reduce costs
  • Provide financial literacy and management training

Expected Impact

10-15% Reduction in early-stage closures (years 0-5)
More Equitable Distribution of business success across neighborhoods
Faster Recovery During economic downturns through proactive support
Data-Informed Policy replacing reactive, crisis-driven interventions
